1 Abstract

Smart phones have become the computing platform to help us lead our modern daily lives. Android devices are physically small devices but otherwise far better equipped with multiple networks, cameras, and sensors than typical desktop computers. That they make phone calls is incidental. That they are on the body of their owners is significant. It is in this context that we should understand the security and privacy issues raised by these devices.

1.1 Abstract 2/4

Current research and development focus is mostly on UI, location/ context based applications, and conservation of battery power, but the layers deep within the mobile platform that handle security and privacy are not as good as they can be.

1.2 Abstract 3/4

We survey the exploits of the last couple of years ranging from arbitrary code execution, Heartbleed to StageFright. In order to understand the technical details behind these, we must also understand not only the internals of the Android mobile OS, Java, Linux, and ARM CPU but also cloud computing and storage, and crowd sourcing.

1.3 Abstract 4/4

In a lab environment, we let the users experience some of the exploits. We present the details of rooting a device, reverse engineering the source code from its APK, and what it takes to design and build a custom ROM that can include proactive forensics.

3 Labs

  1. Each lab experiment has several tasks.
  2. Depending on your available time, and how familiar you are with OS internals and networking, do as many of these tasks as possible.
  3. Setting up your own lab is easy: Linux/Windows PC + Android tools setup details.

4 Lab 1: Get Started

  1. Experience Android as a "full-scale" networked computer system.
  2. Install APKs such as AirDroid, KeepPass, FDroid, and AIDE.
  3. Install Linux-based applications such as bash, tmux, and sftp-server. Build open-source Android apps from downloaded source code bundles.
  4. Create your own HelloThisIsMe.apk.

5 Lab 2: Network Attacks

  1. Attacks that are possible in any LAN or Internet brought to Android.
  2. We install an APK on a rooted device. This APK let us experience ARP and DNS Cache Poisoning on a device that is not required to be rooted.

6 Lab 3: Reverse Engineer an APK

  1. Take apart an APK, and rebuild with patches.
  2. Examine how a ransomware APK worked.
  3. Take a harmless APK and re-build it packed with a Trojan.
